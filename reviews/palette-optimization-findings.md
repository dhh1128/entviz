# entviz palette optimization — findings and trade-offs (v6)

*Source document for the academic-paper revision. Records why the v6 palette
is what it is, what we tried and rejected, and the limits we accept honestly.
Companion figures: `docs/assets/palette-swatch.svg`,
`docs/assets/palette-cvd.svg` (both regenerated by
`scripts/palette_figures.py`). Pinned by `tests/test_v6_palette_lightness.py`.*

Date: 2026-06-02. Triggered by adversarial review finding **F3**
(`reviews/adversarial-2026-06-02.md`): "per-cell edge-color discrimination
collapses under CVD; there is no CVD test."

---

## 1. The problem the palette must solve

entviz uses five colors (`POSSIBLE_EDGE_COLORS`) as the entviz background, the
per-cell surround-ring color, the quartile-mark fill, and the color-bar bands.
Two unrelated values must look unrelated *at a glance*, and a comparing user
must be able to tell two colors apart. "A user" includes people with
color-vision deficiency (CVD), people on monochrome or e-ink displays, and
content viewed through CSS color filters. So the palette's separability cannot
depend on a channel that some viewers lack.

The five colors are **white, gold, red, blue, black**. White and black are
fixed anchors. The interesting question is where gold, red, and blue sit.

## 2. Core principle: lightness is the only robust channel

A color has three perceptual degrees of freedom — **lightness**, **hue**, and
**chroma** (saturation). Under CVD, monochrome rendering, and color filtering,
*hue and chroma are exactly the channels that degrade or vanish*; **lightness
(CIELAB L\*) is the one that survives all of them.** Therefore the palette is
spaced primarily along L\*, and we evaluate separability by **ΔL\*** (lightness
difference), not by a full-color metric.

This is the central design decision and it has a counterintuitive consequence,
covered in §5: the popular "perceptual distance" metric ΔE76 is the *wrong*
optimization target here, because it rewards chroma — the fragile channel.

## 3. The defect we fixed

Through v5 the palette used gold `#ffd966` at **L\*≈88**. The five lightnesses
were white 100, gold 88, red 57, blue 34, black 0. The white→gold gap was only
**ΔL\* ≈ 12** — the smallest separation in the whole palette and well below the
others (gold/red 31, red/blue 23). On a grayscale or achromatopsia rendering,
white and gold were nearly indistinguishable. White/gold was the weak link.

(Note: F3's literal claim was that *gold/red* collapses under deuteranopia.
On investigation that diagnosis was wrong — gold/red is both lightness-separated
*and* hue-backed. The real weak pair was white/gold. F3's underlying concern —
"there is no CVD test and the palette's CVD behavior is unverified" — was valid
and is now addressed.)

## 4. The fix: move gold to the maximin lightness

We held white (100), red (57), blue (34), and black (0) fixed and moved **only
gold's lightness**. Gold sits between white and red, so its two adjacency gaps
are:

- white→gold = `100 − L`
- gold→red  = `L − 57`

Maximizing the *minimum* of these two (the **maximin**) means equalizing them:

```
100 − L = L − 57   ⟹   L = 78.5,   both gaps ≈ 21.5
```

So **L\*78.5 is the optimal gold lightness** — it lifts the palette's
worst-case lightness separation from 12 to ≈21 and makes white/gold and
gold/red equally strong. Pushing gold darker than 78.5 would start sacrificing
gold/red; lighter leaves white/gold weak. This is a true optimum, not a
compromise.

**Why white/gold gets the budget, not gold/red.** gold/red differ in *hue*
(yellow vs red) as well as lightness, so they have a backup cue and tolerate a
smaller lightness gap. White/gold have *no* hue backup that survives CVD (white
is achromatic), so the lightness gap is their only robust separator — that is
where the budget must go.

**Chroma choice.** At L\*78.5 we chose **maximum in-gamut chroma**, giving gold
`#e7be00` (C\*≈80, up from `#ffd966`'s C\*≈60). A stronger, more saturated gold
*reinforces* the gold/red hue cue — the pair that accepted the smaller lightness
gap — at no cost to lightness. The softer-chroma alternative at the same
lightness (`#e3bf4d`, C\*≈60) was considered and rejected in favor of the
stronger hue signal.

**Adopted v6 gold: `#e7be00` (L\*78.4, C\*80).**

## 5. The ΔE76 detour — why we did *not* use it

We initially tried to derive an "optimal" palette by maximizing pairwise
**ΔE76** (Euclidean distance in CIELAB) under CVD simulation. It produced a
palette (gold L\*75 / red L\*50 / blue L\*25) that looked *worse* to the eye,
and the metric made claims that contradicted direct observation:

- On the **protanopia** row, red and blue look nearly identical in lightness,
  yet ΔE76 reported gold/red as the closest pair.
- ΔE76 rated **blue/black** at ≈80 (very far apart) even though blue and black
  are close in lightness — the distance was almost entirely *chroma*.
- The white/gold step looked rational to the eye only on the tritanopia row;
  on every other row ΔE76's white/gold distance looked 2–3× too large.

**Diagnosis:** ΔE76 over-credits chroma. Chroma is precisely the channel that
collapses under CVD/achromatopsia, so "maximize ΔE76" optimizes for the *least*
robust dimension and is actively misleading for accessibility. A higher ΔE76 is
**not** safer. We discarded the ΔE76-optimized palette and the metric, and
optimize on **ΔL\*** instead.

(Two further effects reinforce eye-over-metric here: the **Helmholtz–Kohlrausch
effect** makes saturated colors read lighter than their measured L\*, and the
dark end of the L\* scale compresses perceptually, so a nominally "even" L\*
spacing is not perceptually even. These are reasons the maintainer's
eye-tuned values beat a naive metric optimum, and reasons we validate every
palette change by eye, not by number alone.)

## 6. Rejected alternatives (summary)

| Option | Why rejected |
|---|---|
| Keep `#ffd966` (L\*88) | White/gold ΔL\* ≈ 12 — palette's weak link; white/gold blur under grayscale/achromatopsia. |
| Gold L\*80 `#ecc300` | Marginally better deuteranopia gold/red (ΔL\* 18.3 vs 17.2) but not the maximin; the 1.1 difference is perceptually irrelevant because gold/red has a hue backup. |
| Soft gold `#e3bf4d` (L\*78.5, C\*60) | Same lightness as adopted, but weaker hue cue for the gold/red pair. |
| ΔE76-optimized palette | ΔE76 over-credits the fragile chroma channel; regressed perceptual quality (§5). |
| Re-tune all colors for ΔE ≥ 50 under CVD (F3 option a) | Impossible without abandoning recognizable red/gold/blue (see §7); would invalidate every prior render for a guarantee that can't be met. |

## 7. Honest limits under CVD

Spacing on lightness is robust but **not CVD-proof**. With white/red/blue/black
fixed and gold at the maximin, the unavoidable sub-floor pairs are:

| Vision | Closest pair | ΔL\* | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normal | gold/red | **21.3** | all pairs ≥ 20 (design floor met) |
| Protanopia | red/blue | **7.4** | red darkens onto blue; **no gold choice fixes this** |
| Deuteranopia | gold/red | 17.2 | |
| Tritanopia | red/blue | 15.7 | |

The protanopia red/blue collapse is **inherent**: protan simulation darkens red
until its lightness approaches blue's, and since we are not moving red or blue,
no lightness assignment removes it. These pairs remain distinguishable only via
(a) the retained color axis the specific CVD preserves (e.g. blue–yellow under
protan/deutan), and (b) the **color-bar letters** `w/g/r/b/k` introduced in v6,
which give every viewer a verbal discriminator independent of color. The blank
map and cell text are further color-independent comparison channels.

**Resolution of F3:** we took the honest-downgrade route (F3 option b), not the
re-tune-to-ΔE-50 route (option a). The spec's CVD claim was downgraded from
"even color-blind viewers can detect each cell" to detectable-from-background
with the letters as the guaranteed pairwise discriminator, and the palette is
documented as robust-not-proof. The CVD snapshot test F3 asked for now exists.

| blue | `#2f3fbf` | 34 | bg candidate / edge |

## 9. Methods and citations

- **Lightness / color distance:** CIELAB L\* (CIE 1976), under a D65 white
  point, computed from sRGB via the standard linearization.
- **CVD simulation:** Machado, G. M., Oliveira, M. M., & Fernandes, L. A. F.
  (2009), *A Physiologically-based Model for Simulation of Color Vision
  Deficiency*, IEEE TVCG 15(6). Severity 1.0 (full dichromacy) matrices for
  protan/deutan/tritan.
- **Achromatopsia:** modeled as lightness-only (collapse each color to the gray
  of equal luminance).
- **Helmholtz–Kohlrausch effect:** saturated colors appear lighter than their
  measured luminance; one reason eye-validation beats metric-only optimization.
- All computations are pure-Python and reproduced in
  `scripts/palette_figures.py` and `tests/test_v6_palette_lightness.py`; no
  numerical dependency is required.
